Learning Useful Representations

Generative modelling techniques such as GANs, Diffusion Models or Autoregressive Transformers, have shown unprecedented representational capacity. Although they vary in implementation, they all demonstrate the ability to learn highly complex distributions of image and text data (or both). The result is that we can draw samples from the distribution, either randomly or conditionally given a class or a text prompt. Although the development of these techniques has caused a considerably stir, in particular, for their artistic potential, suitable applications to higher-stakes domains, e.g. medical imaging, are less sensational but equally promising.

One of the most common purported benefits of generative modeling for medical imaging is dataset augmentation or supplementation. Medical datasets are currently much smaller in number and size compared to general purpose datasets. However, this problem may be short-lived, considering the growing value of data science and digital transformations of the health sector. In constrast, the long-term value proposition for these techniques lies in their inherent representaional power; their ability to learn highly expressive relationships and structure within the data. The fact that the models can show us what they have learned, that is, by generating examples, makes them preferable to less transparent models such as image/text classifiers. This work explores this proposition and attempts to motivate further research in the application of generative models in medical imaging.

Adversarial Techniques: GANs to ALAEs

GANs attempt to learn a data distribution implicitly. A generator network, $G$, is trained to generate realistic data from the domain. It is informed by a discriminator network $D$, which learns the difference between real data, and the fake data from the generator. The knowledge in $D$ represents an implicit quality about the data, directing the updates of $G$ to better match the realistic quality. Importantly, there is no explcit constraint for generated data to match real data examples. Consequently, a 'successfully trained' model may be able to generate realistic data, but may be limited in its diversity compared to the real distribution. Ideally we want to be able to explicitly constrain a generator $G$, to produce real data, as well as samples.

Adversarial Latent Autoencoders (ALAEs) provide this contraint, and consequently allow the abiltiy to (1) encode data, $w = E(x)$, where $w$ is the encoding and $x$ is the input data to the encoder network $E$, (2) decoder data, that is, generate data, via $x' = G(w)$, which is effectively the inverse of the previous mapping, and (3) sample from a learned distribtion, where $w = F(z)$, where $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. The aim is that every value of $w$, whether a sample or a real data point, corresponds to a realistic data output, $x'$.
